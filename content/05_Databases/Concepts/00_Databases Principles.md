### **1. Relational Databases (RDBMS)**

These are widely used for structured data and support [[What is SQL|SQL]].

- **Oracle Database** – Popular in large enterprises for its robustness and scalability.
- **[[Microsoft SQL Server]]** – Common in Windows-based environments.
- **MySQL** – Open-source and widely used, especially in web applications.
- **PostgreSQL** – Advanced open-source RDBMS with strong standards compliance.
- **IBM Db2** – Often used in legacy enterprise systems.

---

### **2. NoSQL Databases**

Used for unstructured or semi-structured data, offering flexibility and scalability.

- **MongoDB** – Document-oriented, great for JSON-like data.
- **Cassandra** – Highly scalable, used for handling large volumes of data across many servers.
- **Redis** – In-memory key-value store, often used for caching.
- **Couchbase** – Combines key-value and document database features.
- **Amazon DynamoDB** – Fully managed NoSQL database service by AWS.

---

### **3. NewSQL Databases**

These aim to provide the scalability of NoSQL with the ACID guarantees of traditional RDBMS.

- **Google Cloud Spanner**
- **CockroachDB**
- **TiDB**
- **VoltDB**

---

### **4. Time-Series Databases**

Optimized for time-stamped or time-series data.

- **InfluxDB**
- **TimescaleDB**
- **Prometheus** (often used for monitoring)

---

### **5. Graph Databases**

Used for data with complex relationships.

- **Neo4j**
- **Amazon Neptune**
- **ArangoDB**

---

### **6. Cloud-Native Databases**

Managed services offered by cloud providers.

- **Amazon RDS / Aurora**
- **Google Cloud SQL / Firestore**
- **Azure SQL Database / Cosmos DB**
